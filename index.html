Since 1997, every game of the WNBA has left a trail: draft boards, box scores, attendance counts, award ballots, executive surveys. The archive of Across the Timeline holds nearly three decades of it — a complete record of what the league chose to count.

Numbers feel neutral. They are not. Behind every data point is a decision about what deserves measuring, and behind every ranking is a judgment about who deserves remembering. Our team asks one big question of this archive, through six research questions. Keep scrolling — each one appears over the world it describes.

Team Question 2 · The Draft

What does a top draft pick really buy?

Every spring, one evening reorders the league's future. We follow every top-three pick since 1997 to measure what actually changes afterward — in the standings, and in the stands.

Team Question 3 · The Crowds

Why do some arenas fill while others stay quiet?

Attendance is a nightly vote of support. We compare small cities with big markets to ask which places truly showed up for women's basketball — and why.

Team Question 4 · The Pipeline

Does March Madness stardom survive the jump?

College tournaments crown new heroes every year. The professional game tests them. We trace how NCAA success translates — or doesn't — into professional careers.

Team Question 6 · The Ballots

Do the awards go to the best players?

Awards are the league's official memory. We test MVP votes and expert predictions against the performance data itself — and measure the gap between recognition and reality.

Team Question 8 · The League

How has the league grown — and for whom?

Teams have been born, folded, and relocated; salaries have transformed. Every change redrew the map of opportunity for women in sports. We chart that map across three decades.

Team Question 10 · The Data

Who gets to be called great?

Our final question turns the lens on the numbers themselves: when history is written in rankings and box scores, what definition of greatness gets built in — and what gets left out?

Why this matters

Deciding what to count — and what not to count — is a human choice with consequences, as our data critique explores. A league's data is its memory, and memory is power: it decides which seasons get retold and which quietly disappear. This project reads the WNBA's memory closely, and asks what it reveals — and hides.
