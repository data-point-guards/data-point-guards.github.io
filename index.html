You don't need to know anything about basketball to follow this story. The WNBA is the top professional women's basketball league in the United States, and almost from its first game it has been writing itself down: who was chosen, who came to watch, who won the awards, whose name quietly faded. All of it lives in a public archive called Across the Timeline.

Numbers feel neutral. They are not. Behind every record is a decision about what deserves measuring, and behind every ranking is a judgment about who deserves remembering. This site reads the league's archive as one story, in six short chapters. Keep scrolling — each chapter appears over the world it describes.

The Draft

Where hope gets handed out

Every spring, the teams take turns choosing the best young players in the country — and the teams that struggled most get to choose first. For a losing team and its city, a single lucky pick can change everything: the roster, the mood, even the size of the crowd.

The Crowds

Why do some arenas fill while others stay quiet?

In some cities, tickets are hard to find and the stands roar all night. In others, the same quality of basketball plays to rows of empty seats. The difference says as much about the cities themselves as it does about the teams they cheer for.

The Pipeline

What happens to the college stars?

Every March, women's college basketball takes over American television and turns players into household names. But the professional league is small, with far fewer jobs than there are stars. The leap from campus hero to pro is much harder than it looks — and many never land it.

The Ballots

Do the trophies go to the right players?

At the end of each season, a panel of sportswriters votes on which player mattered most. But experts watch the game through their own eyes, and what they reward does not always match what happened on the court. Sometimes a brilliant season earns everything — and sometimes it earns nothing.

The League

A league that grew, shrank, and grew again

The league began with big ambitions and spread quickly to new cities. Then some teams folded, others moved away, and the survivors held on through the quiet years. Now new cities are lining up to join — and the changing map tells its own story about where women's sports were welcome.

The Data

Who decides what greatness means?

Everything above comes from an archive of records — and records only remember what someone chose to write down. So before trusting the numbers, we ask a harder question: who built them, what do they count, and who do they quietly leave out?

The question behind everything

How does a league decide who matters?

Each of our chapters visits a different room of the same house: the draft where futures are handed out, the arenas where cities show up or don't, the leap from college fame to a professional job, the ballots that crown the best, the map of a league that grew and shrank and grew again — and finally, the archive itself, the quiet keeper of who gets remembered.

Why this matters

Deciding what to count — and what not to count — is a human choice with consequences, as our data critique explores. A league's data is its memory, and memory is power: it decides which seasons get retold and which quietly disappear. This project reads the WNBA's memory closely, and asks what it reveals — and hides.
