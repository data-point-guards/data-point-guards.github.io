Beyond the Box Score: What WNBA Data Reveals — and Hides

Nearly three decades of attendance records, draft results, award votes, and expert surveys tell a story about women's basketball. We asked what the numbers can prove, and what they leave out.

By the Data Point Guards
Digital Humanities 101, UCLA · Summer 2026

This project explores the history of women's professional basketball through Across the Timeline, an archive of WNBA statistics reaching back to the league's first season in 1997 — player transactions, attendance, drafts, awards, and annual surveys of team executives.

Rather than treating these numbers as neutral facts, we approach them with a humanistic lens. Behind every data point is a decision: who gets counted, who gets drafted, who gets recognized, and whose story gets told.

Do teams lose on purpose to win later?

Our first question follows the strategy known as tanking: franchises that accept losing seasons to secure high draft picks. Using draft and performance records, we ask what this gamble costs a team — on the court and at the box office.

Why do some arenas fill while others stay quiet?

Attendance numbers stretch back to 1997, and they are not evenly distributed. We compare teams in smaller cities with those in large metropolitan areas, asking how geography — and competition from other sports — shapes who shows up.

Does March Madness stardom survive the jump to the pros?

College stars arrive in the WNBA with headlines and expectations. By linking NCAA tournament success to professional careers, we trace which reputations hold up — and which quietly fade.

Do the awards go to the best players?

Finally, we test recognition against reality: MVP vote margins against statistical gaps, and General Managers' preseason predictions against how seasons actually ended. When experts and evidence disagree, the voting data shows us where — and invites us to ask why.
