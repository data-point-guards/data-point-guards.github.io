In the spring of 2024, a point guard from West Des Moines, Iowa became the most famous basketball player in America — before playing a single professional game. Caitlin Clark's journey from college phenomenon to WNBA franchise player is a story everyone knows. But it is also something else: a trail of data.

Every chapter of her journey — and of every player's journey since 1997 — was recorded in the archives of Across the Timeline: draft boards, box scores, attendance counts, award ballots, executive surveys. We follow one journey to ask about them all. Keep scrolling.

Chapter 1 · College

A scoring record that stood for 54 years

At Iowa, Clark scored 3,951 career points — more than any Division I player, man or woman, in history. Her games set television records before she ever turned professional.

Chapter 2 · The Stage

March Madness made her a star

Two straight national championship games turned a regional standout into a national story. But does tournament fame survive the jump to the pros? History says: not always.

Chapter 3 · The Draft

April 15, 2024: the No. 1 pick

The Indiana Fever selected Clark first overall — a pick earned by three of the worst seasons in franchise history. Losing, it turns out, can be a strategy.

Chapter 4 · The Crowds

Attendance quadrupled overnight

The Fever drew about 4,000 fans per game in 2023. In 2024, with Clark, more than 17,000 — the highest in the league. One player moved the entire attendance curve.

Chapter 5 · Recognition

How will the league remember her?

Rookie of the Year, All-WNBA, MVP ballots: awards are the league's official memory. We test that memory against the performance data itself — for Clark, and for everyone.

Why this matters

Numbers feel neutral. They are not. Deciding what to count — and what not to count — is a human choice with consequences, as our data critique explores. A league's data is its memory, and memory is power: it decides which journeys get retold and which quietly disappear. This project reads the WNBA's memory closely, and asks what it reveals — and hides.
