01 — The Argument

You do not need to know basketball to feel the weight of a number. Since its first season in 1997, the Women's National Basketball Association has written almost everything down: every draft pick, every ticket sold, every award ballot, every executive's ranking. These records feel neutral, as if they simply report what happened on the court — but they are not. Behind each one is a decision about what deserves to be measured and who deserves to be remembered. This project argues that the league's data is not a mirror of the game but a set of choices about who counts, and that when those choices are read across three decades they reveal a league that has concentrated recognition, opportunity, and survival in a few players, teams, and cities while leaving many others uncounted. Our evidence is Across the Timeline, a public archive of WNBA drafts, attendance, award votes, general-manager surveys, and player movement since 1997 — because it collects in one place the very measures the league uses to define success, it lets us read the league's own scorekeeping against itself.

02 — The Conversation

Scholars have approached these questions from two directions that rarely meet. A tradition in sports economics argues that the numbers fans trust most are often the wrong ones: David Berri shows that a player's true contribution to winning is poorly captured by scoring totals or media votes; Berger and Daumann find that expert evaluators anchor on early reputation rather than real ability; and Agha and Berri show that fan demand in the WNBA follows different rules than in the men's league. A second, humanistic tradition argues that recognition is shaped by culture as much as by performance: Banet-Weiser examines how professional basketball markets images of race and gender, and D'Ignazio and Klein argue that data is never neutral because power decides what gets counted. These literatures rarely meet — and neither has read the WNBA's own archive, across drafts, crowds, careers, ballots, and league history together, as a single story. That is the gap this project enters.

03 — Why It Matters

We study how the WNBA counts its players and teams because we want to find out whose contributions its records reward and whose they quietly erase, so that readers come away understanding that data is never neutral: someone always chooses what to measure, and those choices shape what a sport, and its public, decides to remember. The stakes reach past basketball. The WNBA is a young league still arguing over what its athletes are worth, and the very numbers used in that argument — attendance, awards, salaries, survival — are the ones we examine here. If those measures are partial or biased, the debates built on top of them are too. Reading a league's data closely becomes a way of asking a question that reaches well beyond sports: when an institution records who matters, who gets left off the page?

Six chapters, one question

Each chapter takes up one room of the same house: the leap from college fame to a professional job, the draft where futures are handed out, the arenas where cities show up or stay home, the ballots that crown the best, the map of a league that grew and shrank and grew again, and finally the archive itself — the quiet keeper of who gets remembered. Read them in order, or open any one; each stands on its own.
