Beyond the Box Score

Nearly three decades of WNBA data tell a story about women's basketball — about who gets counted, who gets recognized, and whose story gets told. Scroll to explore.

This project explores the history of women's professional basketball through Across the Timeline, an archive of WNBA statistics reaching back to the league's first season in 1997 — player transactions, attendance, drafts, awards, and annual surveys of team executives.

Numbers feel neutral. They are not. Behind every data point is a decision, and behind every decision is power. We asked four questions of this archive. Keep scrolling — each one appears over the world it describes.

Photo: Wikimedia Commons
Question 01

Do teams lose on purpose to win later?

Tanking: accepting losing seasons to secure high draft picks. We follow the franchises that made this gamble — and what it cost them on the court and at the box office.

Question 02

Why do some arenas fill while others stay quiet?

Attendance records stretch back to 1997, and they are not evenly distributed. We compare small cities with big metropolitan markets to ask how geography shapes who shows up.

Question 03

Does March Madness stardom survive the jump?

College stars arrive in the WNBA with headlines and expectations. We trace which reputations hold up in the pros — and which quietly fade.

Question 04

Do the awards go to the best players?

MVP votes, All-WNBA teams, GM surveys: the league's official memory of who mattered. We test that memory against the performance data itself.

The field

Recognition drops fast

From third place down, votes collapse. Great seasons can disappear from the league's official memory almost entirely. That gap — between performance and recognition — is what our project measures.
