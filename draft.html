Team Research Question 2

What does a top draft pick really buy?

A high draft pick is the biggest prize a struggling franchise can win. But does it actually pay off — in the standings, in the stands, and on the broadcast? The Indiana Fever ran the experiment — and got Caitlin Clark.

Our research question: “How does securing top three draft picks correlate with changes in home game attendance and broadcast viewership over the subsequent three seasons?” — and does the acquisition of a single elite draft pick translate into improved team metrics (net rating / shooting percentage)?

From 2020 to 2022, the Indiana Fever won fewer games than any team in the league. Those losses became lottery odds, and the lottery odds became the No. 1 overall pick — twice in a row: Aliyah Boston in 2023, then Caitlin Clark in 2024. What followed is the best recent test of our question: within one season the Fever's wins bounced back, and their home attendance quadrupled to the highest in the league.

The valley before the peak
Indiana Fever wins per season · Source: Across the Timeline (schedules and standings)

The chart shows the shape of a rebuild: three seasons at the bottom, then the bounce after two No. 1 picks. But one team's success story is not proof that top picks always pay off. For every Fever, the archive holds franchises that landed high picks and never converted them into wins or crowds — and that is exactly what our full analysis will measure.

What we will investigate

Using draft data, standings, and attendance records since 1997, we will follow every top-three draft pick in league history and measure what changed in the three seasons that followed: the team's wins, its home attendance, and — where data exists — its television viewership. We will also isolate the single-elite-pick cases to test whether one player visibly improves team performance metrics such as net rating and shooting percentage, or whether it takes more than one draft night to change a franchise. One honest limitation: broadcast viewership is not part of our core dataset, so that part of the question relies on secondary sources — a gap our data critique discusses.

Chart data is preliminary and will be verified against our cleaned dataset. Analysis and final visualizations by our team are in progress.
