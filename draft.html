Team Research Question 2

What does a top draft pick really buy?

A high draft pick is the biggest prize a struggling franchise can win. But does it actually pay off — in the standings, in the stands, and on the broadcast? The Indiana Fever ran the experiment — and got Caitlin Clark.

Our research question: “How does securing top three draft picks correlate with changes in home game attendance and broadcast viewership over the subsequent three seasons?” — and does the acquisition of a single elite draft pick translate into improved team metrics (net rating / shooting percentage)?

From 2020 to 2022, the Indiana Fever won fewer games than any team in the league. Those losses became lottery odds, and the lottery odds became the No. 1 overall pick — twice in a row: Aliyah Boston in 2023, then Caitlin Clark in 2024. What followed is the best recent test of our question: within one season the Fever's wins bounced back, and their home attendance quadrupled to the highest in the league.

The valley before the peak

The chart shows the shape of a rebuild: three seasons at the bottom, then the bounce after two No. 1 picks. But wins are only half of our question. The other half is the box office — and there, the same three seasons tell an even more dramatic story.

The box-office payoff: attendance around the No. 1 picks
Indiana Fever average home attendance, the season before and the two seasons of No. 1 picks · Source: league reports (preliminary)

In 2022, before either pick, the Fever drew fewer than two thousand fans per game. The first No. 1 pick roughly doubled the crowd; the second multiplied it four times over, to the best attendance in the league. For a franchise's finances, this is the real prize of a top pick: not just a better team, but a reason for a city to show up. Our full analysis will test whether this pattern repeats across all top-three picks in league history — or whether the Fever's case is a spectacular exception.

There is also a warning hidden in the history of the draft. A top pick is a lottery ticket, not a guarantee: across the league's history, most No. 1 overall selections never became MVP-level players, and some played only a handful of seasons. The pick creates the possibility of transformation — the player has to deliver it, and stay healthy through it.

The odds behind the hype
Of the 29 players drafted No. 1 overall (1997–2025), how many went on to win MVP · Source: Across the Timeline drafts and awards (preliminary)

Eight of twenty-nine — a little more than one in four. Being drafted first is the strongest single predictor of stardom the league has, and even it fails most of the time. Meanwhile, MVP trophies have also gone to second picks, late first-rounders, and players the draft barely noticed. The draft is the league's loudest promise about who will matter; the rest of a career is a test of that promise.

What we will investigate

Using draft data, standings, and attendance records since 1997, we will follow every top-three draft pick in league history and measure what changed in the three seasons that followed: the team's wins, its home attendance, and — where data exists — its television viewership. We will also isolate the single-elite-pick cases to test whether one player visibly improves team performance metrics such as net rating and shooting percentage, or whether it takes more than one draft night to change a franchise. One honest limitation: broadcast viewership is not part of our core dataset, so that part of the question relies on secondary sources — a gap our data critique discusses.

Why it matters

The draft is where the league most openly treats players as assets: ranked, selected, traded, and priced by expectation. Asking what a pick “buys” a franchise makes that logic visible — and asking whether the purchase pays off in crowds and wins lets the data answer a question usually settled by hype. When a city's support for women's basketball can quadruple because of a single draft night, the draft is not just a sports event; it is an engine that decides which cities get to fall in love with this league.
