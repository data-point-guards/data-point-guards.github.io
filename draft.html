Question 01

Do teams lose on purpose to win later?

The strategy is called tanking: accept losing seasons, collect high draft picks, and bet the franchise's future on them. The Indiana Fever ran the experiment — and got Caitlin Clark.

Research question by Ryu Ohnogi

From 2020 to 2022, the Indiana Fever won fewer games than any team in the league. Whether by design or misfortune, those losses became lottery odds, and the lottery odds became the No. 1 overall pick — twice in a row: Aliyah Boston in 2023, then Caitlin Clark in 2024. Within one season, the Fever went from the league's basement to its biggest story.

The valley before the peak
Indiana Fever wins per season · Source: Across the Timeline (schedules and standings)

The chart shows the shape of a rebuild: three seasons at the bottom, then the bounce. But one team's success story is not proof that the strategy works. For every Fever, the archive holds franchises that lost for years and never converted the picks into wins — and that is exactly what our full analysis will measure.

What we will investigate

Following Ryu's research question, we will use draft data, standings, and transaction records since 1997 to follow every team that finished at the bottom of the league: how long they stayed there, what they did with their picks, and whether the losing ever paid off. Specifically: how securing a top-three pick correlates with home attendance over the following three seasons, and whether a single elite pick actually improves team performance metrics. Because “tanking” is hard to define objectively, we measure outcomes of losing seasons rather than accusing any team of losing on purpose.

Chart data is preliminary and will be verified against our cleaned dataset. Analysis and final visualizations by our team are in progress.
