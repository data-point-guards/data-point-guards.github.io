Critical Analysis

Reading the Dataset Against the Grain

Before trusting any dataset, ask who made it, what it counts, and what it leaves out. Our team examined Across the Timeline from six angles.

What information is included in the dataset?

The “Across the Timeline” website contains multiple datasets related to women’s basketball, with the main focus being on the women’s professional league, the WNBA. The data includes player transactions (such as trades and free agency signings), attendance records, box scores, schedules, standings, draft results, awards, All-Star selections, and top player rankings. The datasets are organized in manner that is easy to navigate, allowing users to search across categories such as players, teams, seasons, leagues, and historical records which may be joined after further analysis. There is also a section with General Manager surveys, which includes information about how front offices evaluate other players and teams. The data covers the history of the WNBA going back to 1997, when the league began its first season. There is also data that goes beyond the WNBA that includes women’s college basketball such as AP rankings and tournament results. Overall, the website contains a wide collection of women’s basketball data that includes both game-related statistics and historical league records.

— Nguyen Ngo

Ideological effects of how the dataset is organized and divided

The way the dataset is organized and divided has several ideological effects. First, organizing player information into categories such as transactions, drafts, and rankings treats players as assets to be traded, measured, and evaluated, which reflects the business viewpoint of team front offices more than the perspectives of the players themselves. Second, dividing the data into sections like GM Surveys and Top Players gives authority to executives and experts in deciding whose opinions matter and which players deserve recognition. Third, because the dataset presents history through statistics, records, and rankings, it encourages users to understand the sport mainly through competition, achievement, and comparison. Finally, separating the WNBA, college basketball, and newer leagues such as Unrivaled and Athletes Unlimited into distinct sections creates a hierarchy that positions the professional league as the standard of the sport. Overall, the organization shapes users to see women’s basketball as something quantifiable and managed.

— Yuan Gao
