Critical Analysis

Reading the Dataset Against the Grain

Before trusting any dataset, ask who made it, what it counts, and what it leaves out. Our team examined Across the Timeline from six angles.

What information is included in the dataset?

The “Across the Timeline” website contains multiple datasets related to women’s basketball, with the main focus being on the women’s professional league, the WNBA. The data includes player transactions (such as trades and free agency signings), attendance records, box scores, schedules, standings, draft results, awards, All-Star selections, and top player rankings. The datasets are organized in a manner that is easy to navigate, allowing users to search across categories such as players, teams, seasons, leagues, and historical records which may be joined after further analysis. There is also a section with General Manager surveys, which includes information about how front offices evaluate other players and teams. The data covers the history of the WNBA going back to 1997, when the league began its first season. There is also data that goes beyond the WNBA that includes women’s college basketball such as AP rankings and tournament results. Overall, the website contains a wide collection of women’s basketball data that includes both game-related statistics and historical league records.

What can the dataset reveal or illuminate?

Using datasets such as attendance, schedules, and standings, we can map out the popularity of the WNBA over time, exploring whether attendance spiked after specific draft classes and how dynasty teams impact attendance in their home cities versus away games. We can also analyze how “successful” WNBA draft picks are by checking what percentage of first-round picks go on to become All-Stars or win championships, which could illuminate whether “tanking” actually pays off in the long run for franchises. Furthermore, comparing subjective data like GM surveys and award voting against objective performance statistics could reveal hidden biases in how certain players are perceived and evaluated by the media. Finally, by examining player transactions and career data, we can track the volatility of WNBA careers, measuring how the league’s strictly limited roster spots impact the frequency of trades and the average longevity of players over time.

Ideological effects of how the dataset is organized and divided

The way the dataset is organized and divided has several ideological effects. First, organizing player information into categories such as transactions, drafts, and rankings treats players as assets to be traded, measured, and evaluated, which reflects the business viewpoint of team front offices more than the perspectives of the players themselves. Second, dividing the data into sections like GM Surveys and Top Players gives authority to executives and experts in deciding whose opinions matter and which players deserve recognition. Third, because the dataset presents history through statistics, records, and rankings, it encourages users to understand the sport mainly through competition, achievement, and comparison. Finally, separating the WNBA, college basketball, and newer leagues such as Unrivaled and Athletes Unlimited into distinct sections creates a hierarchy that positions the professional league as the standard of the sport. Overall, the organization shapes users to see women’s basketball as something quantifiable and managed.

How was the data generated, and what are the original sources?

The data set was generated during seasonal games hosted by the WNBA and NCAA. It is important to note, the NCAA games were filtered to only explore Division One bracketed Women’s basketball games. The collection of this data was founded in 2018 and is still operational today in 2026. The original sources of the data are mainly collected by two methods. The first method is collection through player agencies, such as the agreements and contracts that the players themselves are bound to. This can include a quota for play time per game and attendance or physical requirements in addition to contract length with their respective teams. The second methodology stems from team statistics. These are gathered through means of scraping official WNBA or NCAA league documents and websites, in addition to analyzing game box scores through live and recorded digital media.

Who created and funded the dataset, and what information is left out?

The dataset Across the timeline was created by Across the Timeline LLC. The website does not clearly identify who created or funds it. Under the newsletter it mentions this project started in 2018 and asks for donations to have the website running and continue to be free. The dataset focuses on the history of the WNBA such as players transactions, drafts, awards, top players, etc. However, it does not include all of basketball’s history or every historical basketball event such as men’s basketball or sports outside women’s basketball. Another thing to add is that it does not include player interviews, financial analytics, or explanations to why the players are traded.

What can the dataset not reveal?

While Across the Timeline gives a lot of useful data, it cannot fully explain the reasons behind the numbers. For example, the transactions data can show when a player was traded, signed, or waived, but it cannot show the full reasons behind those moves, such as contract issues, injuries, team decisions, or personal reasons. The attendance data can show whether more or fewer people went to games, but it does not explain why attendance changed, such as marketing, ticket prices, media coverage, or local fan interest. Also, awards, GM surveys, and top-player rankings show how players were recognized, but they do not fully reveal bias, popularity, or how players were viewed outside official records. If this dataset were our only source, we would mostly see WNBA history through measurable events and rankings, but not the full human context or larger reasons behind them.
