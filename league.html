Question 05

How has the league grown — and for whom?

A player's journey is only possible where a league exists to receive it. The WNBA has expanded, contracted, and expanded again — and every change redrew the map of opportunity for women in sports.

Research question by Mariana Rosas

In 1997, eight teams played the WNBA's first season. The league nearly doubled by 2000, then shrank through a decade of folded and relocated franchises — the Houston Comets won four straight championships and still disappeared. For fifteen years the league held at twelve teams. Now it is growing again: Golden State in 2025, Toronto and Portland in 2026.

Three decades of expansion and contraction
Number of WNBA teams by season · Source: Across the Timeline (preliminary)

Each team is roughly twelve roster spots: when the league contracts, careers end overnight; when it expands, doors open. But opportunity is not only about roster spots — it is about whether a player can afford to stay. For most of league history, salaries were low enough that stars spent every offseason playing overseas. The 2020 collective bargaining agreement began to change that.

What we will investigate

Following Mariana's question, we will trace how the league's growth changed opportunities for women in sports over time: how many players got to have WNBA careers in each era, what happened to the players of folded franchises (visible in the transaction records), and how economic conditions — salaries, roster limits, the overseas offseason economy — shaped players' decisions. We will also examine her question about positions: whether some positions are drafted higher, paid better, and recognized more than others, and what that hierarchy means for who becomes a star.

Salary information is not included in our core dataset — a gap our data critique discusses — so that part of the analysis relies on secondary sources. Chart data is preliminary and will be verified.
