Team Research Question 4

Do the awards go to the best players?

Every October, seventy-two ballots decide how a season will be remembered. We put the league's official memory on trial — against the performance record itself.

Our research question: “How well do awards and expert opinions in the WNBA reflect players’ actual performance on the court, and what might explain the gaps between recognition and reality?”

A basketball season produces two kinds of records. One is written by the games themselves — points, rebounds, wins — and accumulates in box scores that no one disputes. The other is written by people: sportswriters filling out award ballots, executives answering preseason surveys, voters deciding in a single October week which names a season will be remembered by. Our question lives in the space between the two records. When they agree, recognition looks like measurement. When they disagree, something else is being measured — and this page is our attempt to catch that something in the act.

Sometimes the two records align perfectly. In 2024, the Rookie of the Year vote was as close to unanimity as voting allows — sixty-six of sixty-seven first-place votes for one player. Held beside what follows, it is the exception that makes the rule visible.

Who receives the votes — and who barely does

Sub-question: “To what extent do WNBA award votes reflect players’ actual season performance, and what patterns can we find in which players receive more votes?”

Begin with the full ballot of the 2025 MVP race — not the headline, but every player who received a single vote.

The 2025 MVP vote, complete: thirteen names, one cliff
Total voting points, all thirteen players who received votes · Source: Across the Timeline (award votes) · From our individual data visualization assessment

A'ja Wilson won with 657 points, carrying 51 of the 72 first-place votes; Napheesa Collier finished second with 534. Read closely, the top of the race was tighter than the result suggests — the 123 points separating Wilson from Collier are fewer than the 143 separating Collier from Alyssa Thomas in third. It is below the top three that the ballot falls off a cliff. Allisha Gray received 180 points and Kelsey Mitchell 93, though both anchored playoff teams; beyond the top five, no player in the league received more than 5 points, and five players received exactly one fifth-place vote apiece. In a league of roughly 156 roster spots, thirteen names appeared on the ballot at all. Recognition, the chart says, is not distributed — it is concentrated, pooled around one or two agreed-upon stars while everyone else splits the residue. Whether that concentration mirrors performance is the question the rest of this page pursues.

Recognition against reality

Sub-question: “How do the differences between MVP and other WNBA award winners compare with their seasonal metrics, and what can this tell us about how voters make decisions?”

The sharpest test we can run puts every substantial 2025 season on a single canvas: each of the 42 players who scored at least 400 points, placed by what she produced (horizontally) and what the voters gave her (vertically). If recognition simply followed production, the dots would climb together in a rough line. Hover over any dot to see who she is.

Scoring vs. recognition, 2025: two different worlds
Season total points vs. MVP voting points, all 42 players with 400+ points · Orange = received votes, gray = none · Source: Across the Timeline · From our individual data visualization assessment

The dots do not climb together. Instead the chart splits into two worlds. Along the bottom runs a long gray row of production without recognition: Kelsey Plum scored 839 points — the fourth-highest total in the league — and received zero votes. Dearica Hamby scored 811 and received exactly one. Kelsey Mitchell, second in the league in scoring at 890, collected one-seventh of the winner's total. Above them, a small orange constellation rises: Wilson (937 points, 657 votes), Collier (755, 534), Thomas (599, 391). Thomas is the figure to sit with — she received four times Mitchell's votes while scoring nearly 300 fewer points, which tells us the voters reward things the scoring column cannot see: playmaking, defense, a team's success. That is a defensible way to vote. But the chart also prices it: score brilliantly for a losing or unfashionable team, and you become nearly invisible in the league's official memory.

None of this is unique to the WNBA, which is precisely why it is worth measuring. In the study that founded this line of inquiry, the sports economist David Berri built a model linking each NBA player's statistics to the wins she actually produces, then compared the model's verdicts with the ones voters and executives reach — and found the two regularly part ways. Perceived value, Berri concluded, is not measured value. Our scatter plot carries his question into a league where no one had systematically asked it. One honest caveat travels with it: total points partly reflect games played and role, so our full analysis will rerun this comparison with additional metrics — efficiency, rebounds, assists — before drawing final conclusions.

The people in power

Sub-question: “How accurate have the WNBA GM survey predictions been compared to the actual season results across league history? What biases do they reveal of people in ‘power’?”

Sportswriters vote on awards, but a second panel of experts shapes careers more directly: the General Managers who draft, trade, and pay every player in the league. Each preseason, the league surveys them and publishes the results — which means their judgment, unlike most expert opinion, is on the record and gradeable. Their 2026 predictions are already filed.

“Who will win the 2026 MVP?” — the executives, on the record
Share of General Managers picking each player, 2026 preseason survey · Source: Across the Timeline (GM surveys)

Sixty percent of the league's executives converged on a single name — the same concentration of expectation the award ballots showed. Convergence is not foresight, and the scholarship gives reasons for doubt: studying NBA front offices, Tobias Berger and Frank Daumann demonstrated that the professionals who evaluate basketball players for a living anchor on first impressions — draft position, early reputation — and cling to them after the evidence has moved on. Expert confidence, their work shows, is not the same thing as expert accuracy. Across three decades of archived surveys, we can measure exactly how often the WNBA's most powerful evaluators were right, whom they repeatedly overrated, and whom they never saw coming.

What explains the gaps

Counting the gaps between recognition and reality is the measurable half of our question; explaining them is the humanistic half. Voting is a cultural act performed by people embedded in a media economy, and that economy has never treated all players equally. In her study of the WNBA's founding years, Sarah Banet-Weiser showed that race and gender have structured which basketball players get valued — and in what terms — since the league's first season, with marketing and media coverage elevating certain kinds of stars while rendering others peripheral. The gaps in our charts, in other words, have cultural roots older than any single ballot. When we ask why Kelsey Plum's 839 points earned zero votes while a comparable season elsewhere earned hundreds, the answer will not be found in the box score alone — which is exactly why this project pairs statistical analysis with critical reading.

What we will investigate

With complete award votes, GM survey answers, and season statistics joined together, we will measure the recognition gap across the league's full history: whether vote margins track statistical margins, whether voters favor winning teams and past winners, how often the GMs' preseason predictions came true, and which players — and kinds of players — the official memory has repeatedly overlooked. Full citations for the scholarship on this page are on our Sources page.

Why it matters

Awards are how a league writes its own history. Decades from now, casual fans will not re-read box scores; they will read lists of MVPs and All-WNBA teams, and those lists will stand in for the truth. If the votes systematically favor certain teams, styles, or stories, then the league's official memory is quietly biased — and players who performed brilliantly in the wrong uniform simply vanish from it. Measuring the gap between recognition and reality is our way of auditing that memory while the evidence still exists.
