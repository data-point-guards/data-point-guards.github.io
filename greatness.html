The Data

Who gets to be called great?

“How does the dataset’s focus on rankings, awards, and team statistics influence the way we define ‘greatness’ in women’s basketball?”

A dataset is not a mirror; it is a lens. Across the Timeline records what can be counted — points, wins, votes, rankings — and whatever the numbers capture becomes the raw material of reputation. So before trusting the archive's verdicts, we asked it a simple question: when the league names its five “greatest” players of 2025, is it naming its five best scorers?

From the ballot to the box score
Each line is one of the 2025 MVP top five: her rank in the vote (left) connected to her rank in scoring (right, among 42 players with 400+ points) · Flat lines mean the ballot matched the box score; steep lines mean it did not · Source: Across the Timeline

The answer is no — and the mismatch is the finding. The MVP and the runner-up spot did not go to the two leading scorers: Napheesa Collier finished second in the voting while ranking seventh in scoring, and Alyssa Thomas finished third while ranking sixteenth. Meanwhile the league's second-leading scorer, Kelsey Mitchell, placed only fifth in the vote. Voters clearly reward something the scoring column does not measure: Thomas is the league's premier playmaker, Collier led the team with the league's best record, and both anchor their teams' defense. Points alone do not make greatness — team success and synchronicity seem to matter as much or more.

There is a second pattern worth seeing: how few players the word “great” is allowed to touch at all. In 2025, roughly 156 players held WNBA roster spots. Thirteen received even a single MVP vote.

How many players does "greatness" reach?
Of roughly 156 WNBA roster spots in 2025, players receiving at least one MVP vote · Source: Across the Timeline award votes (preliminary)

Under one player in twelve. Everyone else — the defenders, the screeners, the bench units that win championships in April — exists in the dataset only as rows that received nothing. The archive doesn't say they weren't great; it simply has no ballot line where their kind of greatness could be recorded.

And here is the humanistic turn: the dataset itself cannot fully show what it leaves out. It has no column for leadership, for defensive gravity, for the screens that free a shooter, or for the trust between teammates. When we — or the voters — call someone great, we are partly measuring and partly believing. A dataset built on rankings and box scores quietly teaches its users that greatness is whatever the box score can hold.

What we will investigate

Using award votes, season statistics, and standings together, we will measure how much of award recognition is explained by scoring, how much by team record, and how much by defensive and playmaking statistics — across the league's full history, not just one season. Where the numbers stop explaining, we will turn to our data critique: the gaps in this dataset are not accidents, and what it cannot count shapes what the sport remembers.

Scoring ranks are computed from the same verified 2025 dataset used on the Awards page. Analysis and final visualizations by our team are in progress.
