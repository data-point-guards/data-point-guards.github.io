Team Research Question 3

As submitted: “How does NCAA tournament success translate into a WNBA career?” — do college stars maintain their statistics or deplete after being drafted, how are collegiate players valued, and are drafted stars more susceptible to injury and shorter careers?

Does March Madness stardom survive the jump?

Every spring, the NCAA tournament crowns new stars. Every summer, the WNBA tests them. The two datasets meet at the draft — and don't always agree.

By the time she left Iowa, Caitlin Clark had scored more points than anyone in the history of major college basketball — men's or women's. The record she broke had stood since 1970.

But college greatness is a promise, not a guarantee. The history of the draft is full of tournament heroes who faded in the professional game, and of overlooked role players who became champions. Clark delivered on the promise immediately; many before her did not.

And even for those who make it, the question becomes how long they can last. The professional game is faster, more physical, and less forgiving than college — most WNBA careers are measured in a handful of seasons, not decades. Yet some No. 1 picks played on for fifteen years or more.

What we will investigate

We will link each drafted player's college and her school's NCAA tournament runs (Sweet 16 and beyond) to complete WNBA careers, and test each part of our question in turn. First, translation: do players from deep tournament runs have longer, better professional careers, or does March fame inflate draft position beyond what the professional game rewards? Second, depletion: do college stars maintain their statistics after the draft, or do the numbers fall as they move from being the centerpiece of a college offense to one professional among twelve? The transition is brutal by design — a rookie arrives weeks after her college season ends, joins a shorter roster of stronger athletes, and plays a faster game with a deeper three-point line. Third, valuation: which colleges the league trusts, visible in three decades of draft boards, and whether that trust is earned by the programs or merely by their television exposure.

Finally, durability. The career-length data lets us ask whether the players who arrive with the heaviest college workloads and the highest expectations are also the ones whose careers end soonest — whether injury and accumulated wear, the invisible cost of two thousand college minutes a year, show up later as shortened professional lives. The chart above already hints that even No. 1 picks live very different career lengths; the full analysis will ask what separates the nineteen-year careers from the three-year ones.

Why it matters

The college-to-pro pipeline is where the sport decides whose story continues. Every spring, dozens of decorated college players simply stop appearing in the data — not because they stopped being good, but because the league has fewer than 160 jobs. Asking how tournament success translates is really asking how fair that filter is: whether it rewards sustained excellence or one famous March, and whether the players asked to carry the college game's popularity pay for it later with their bodies. A league is only as healthy as its pipeline is honest.

Individual college statistics are not part of our core dataset, so this chapter works at the level of schools and tournament results, supplemented by NCAA records where needed. Chart data is preliminary and will be verified.
